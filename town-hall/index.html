01 — What is Figma

Let me tell you about Figma.

Figma launched in 2016 and quickly disrupted an industry. Before it, designers worked in Photoshop. Then XD. Tools that lived on your hard drive, didn't sync, and required a 20-step handoff process just to share a button with a developer.

Figma was browser-based, collaborative, and real-time. You could share a link and anyone could open it and see exactly what you were looking at. No exports. No version confusion.

For designers, it felt like a revolution. And for a while, it was. If you worked in product or brand design between 2017 and 2023, Figma was the job. Understanding it deeply was a career skill. It was the tool that separated designers who knew what they were doing from designers who didn't.

02 — The Adobe Moment

Adobe tried to buy it
for $20 billion.

In 2022, Adobe announced it would acquire Figma for $20 billion. The largest design software acquisition in history.

Adobe had Photoshop, Illustrator, XD. It had decades of industry dominance. And it still felt the need to spend $20 billion to neutralize a 6-year-old startup.

European regulators blocked the deal in late 2023.

BUT the real threat to Figma was never Adobe. It was something else entirely.

03 — Two Things I Built

Each one
chips away.

The best way to understand where design is going isn't to read about it. It's to look at what's already being built. Here are two things I've shipped at RVezy that didn't exist in a designer's toolkit two years ago.

01

The Email Builder

Our email production workflow is ridiculous: design in Figma, export with Emailify (the worst plugin ever built), hand off to Braze, fix everything that broke. In just a few days I built an internal Next.js tool that replaced that entire chain. Pick a template, configure blocks, preview in dark and light mode, and copy the HTML — without touching Figma or writing a single line of code. It's live and we're iterating to make it production-ready.

02

The Figma Plugin — and the Brain Behind It

Before Stitch, I built an AI-powered Figma plugin that generates production-ready Vue and Tailwind components pulled from our design system.

Behind it are two files: a design system document that captures every component, color, spacing rule, and interaction pattern RVezy uses in its product today. Plus a color registry that maps our brand colors into values any tool or model can read.

Without Figma and this plugin, those two files are what RVezy's design language actually looks like. That's the real next step.

04 — How I Work Now

Figma is the
last step.

Let me walk you through how I actually explore a new design idea today. Two tools. Neither of them are Figma.

Tailwind CSS is a framework developers use to style websites.

Stitch is a Google Labs tool that turns a plain English description into a working UI. It runs entirely in your browser. It takes minutes, not hours. And when you're happy with a direction, you paste it straight into Figma.

So here's the workflow: find a component in Tailwind → describe in Stitch → refine → paste to Figma. Figma is still there. But it's no longer where ideas are born. It's where they get finalized.

05 — So Is Design Dead?

The tool was
never the skill.

A designer's career has historically been built on being very good at specific software. Figma. Photoshop before that. XD in between. Job descriptions used to include the tools by name.

So when I say Figma is losing relevance, I'm saying it as someone whose identity was tied to it.

But here's what I've come to understand: the designers who are thriving right now aren't the ones who resisted these tools — they're the ones who recognized what actually made them good in the first place. It was never really about the software.

AI can generate a UI in seconds, but it cannot tell you whether that UI respects your user. That part still requires a human.

The role is changing. The canvas is changing. The tools are absolutely changing. But the reason great design matters hasn't changed at all. If anything, in a world flooded with AI-generated interfaces, taste is becoming rarer and more valuable, not less.
